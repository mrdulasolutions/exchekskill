# Deemed Export Best Practices: 15 CFR 734.2(b) and Related Provisions

Reference for conducting a deemed export / foreign national review under the EAR. Use this to structure the analysis and memo. Cite 15 CFR parts/sections in the memo where relevant.

---

## 1. 15 CFR 734.2(b) — Deemed export

- **Definition:** A “release” of “technology” or “source code” to a **foreign national** in the United States is “deemed” to be an **export** to the country (or countries) of which the foreign national is a **citizen** or a **permanent resident**.
- **Relevance:** The “destination” for license and Country Chart purposes is the foreign national’s citizenship or permanent residence, not a physical shipment.
- **Scope:** Applies to technology and source code subject to the EAR. Physical items (equipment) are exported when shipped; deemed export rules focus on **release of technical knowledge or code** to a foreign national (e.g., through access, training, visual inspection, or disclosure).
- **Citation:** 15 C.F.R. § 734.2(b).

---

## 2. Foreign national vs. U.S. person

- **Foreign national:** Any person who is **not** a “U.S. person” as defined in the EAR (see Part 772).
- **U.S. person (for EAR):** Includes (1) U.S. citizens, (2) lawful permanent residents (green card holders), (3) protected individuals (e.g., asylees, refugees under 8 U.S.C. 1324b), (4) entities organized under U.S. law and certain U.S. branches. See 15 C.F.R. § 772.1 (“U.S. person”).
- **Why it matters:** If the recipient is a U.S. person, there is **no deemed export** (no “foreign” destination). If the recipient is a foreign national, the “destination” for the deemed export is the country of citizenship or permanent residence.
- **Dual nationals:** A person may hold citizenship in more than one country. For deemed export, BIS has treated the country of **citizenship** (and sometimes **permanent residence**) as the destination(s). When in doubt or when both countries are sensitive, **recommend counsel**.
- **Ambiguous status:** If nationality or immigration status is unclear, note in the memo and consider **recommend counsel**.

---

## 3. Technology vs. software / source code

- **Technology (Part 772):** Information necessary for the “development,” “production,” or “use” of an item. It can be tangible (documents, specs) or intangible (training, know-how). 15 C.F.R. § 772.1.
- **Source code:** A subset of technology when it conveys technical information (e.g., for development or use of software or equipment). Encryption source code has specific rules (e.g., 5D002, License Exception TSU). 15 C.F.R. § 772.1.
- **Software (as “technology”):** Object code or executable software can still be a “release” of technology when it conveys technical knowledge (e.g., detailed design, algorithms). Mass-market and publicly available software may be carved out under § 734.3(b)(3) or § 734.7.
- **Use in memo:** State whether the release is “technology,” “source code,” or both; cite Part 772 definitions briefly. If classification (ECCN) is known, state it; otherwise note “classification recommended.”

---

## 4. Fundamental research (15 CFR 734.8 and 734.11)

- **734.8:** Information that is “published” and “ordinarily published” is not subject to the EAR. “Fundamental research” under 734.8 is research in science/engineering the results of which are ordinarily published and shared broadly (no publication restrictions, no government restrictions on participation).
- **734.11:** “Fundamental research” is defined as basic and applied research in science/engineering at an accredited U.S. institution of higher education where the resulting information is ordinarily published; or similar research elsewhere that meets the same criteria (no restrictions on publication or participation).
- **Effect:** If the technology or software is **fundamental research** (and not subject to any restriction that would remove it from the fundamental-research exclusion), it may be **outside EAR control** for deemed-export purposes. Document the rationale (e.g., university project, no publication restrictions).
- **Caveats:** (1) Sponsor or government restrictions on publication or foreign national participation can remove the activity from the fundamental-research exclusion. (2) Pre-publication or proprietary results may still be controlled. (3) When borderline, **recommend counsel**.

---

## 5. Other carve-outs (no “release” or not subject to EAR)

- **Public domain / publicly available (734.7):** Information that is “published,” “generally accessible,” or “released at an open conference” may be outside EAR. See 15 C.F.R. § 734.7. If the information is already public, there may be no “release” requiring a license for deemed export.
- **Educational (740.9 TSU):** License Exception TSU permits export of certain “educational information” and “mass market” encryption. For deemed export, TSU and other exceptions still apply to the “destination” (country of nationality).
- **Use in memo:** If the user asserts public domain, fundamental research, or another carve-out, document the basis and cite the section. If uncertain, state “recommend counsel” or “classification/counsel recommended.”

---

## 6. License and exception for deemed export

- **Destination:** For deemed export, the “destination” is the country of the foreign national’s **citizenship** or **permanent residence** (not the physical location of the release).
- **Country Chart and license requirement:** Apply the Commerce Country Chart (15 C.F.R. Part 738) and license requirements to that country. If a license is required and no exception applies, state “License required” or “Deemed export applies — license required.”
- **License exceptions:** Exceptions in Part 740 (e.g., TSU, TMP, CIV) apply to deemed exports when the terms are met for that “destination.” Document which exception (if any) applies and any restrictions (e.g., 740.2 eligibility).
- **When to recommend license vs. exception vs. counsel:** If the destination is embargoed or highly restricted, or the ECCN is highly controlled (e.g., 9x515, 600 series), recommend license or counsel. If a clear exception applies (e.g., TSU for eligible mass-market encryption), document it. If borderline, **recommend counsel**.

---

## 7. When to say “Recommend counsel”

Recommend counsel when:

- Nationality, citizenship, or permanent residence is ambiguous or dual.
- The technology/software is highly controlled (e.g., 9x515, 600 series, sensitive encryption) and the destination is sensitive.
- Fundamental research or another carve-out is asserted but facts are borderline (e.g., publication restrictions, sponsor controls).
- Company policy requires legal review for deemed exports.
- The user or the facts suggest enforcement or prior violation concerns.

State briefly in the memo: “Recommend counsel” and one or two reasons (e.g., “Dual nationality and sensitive ECCN”; “Fundamental research eligibility unclear”).

---

## 8. Memo conclusion (three outcomes)

| Conclusion | When to use |
|------------|-------------|
| **Deemed export does not apply** | Recipient is a U.S. person; or the information is not subject to the EAR (e.g., fundamental research, public domain); or there is no “release” of technology/source code. |
| **Deemed export applies** | Release of technology/source code to a foreign national; item is subject to the EAR; no carve-out. Then state: license required, or exception (e.g., TSU) applies with citation. |
| **Recommend counsel** | Ambiguous facts, dual nationals, sensitive ECCN or destination, borderline fundamental research, or policy requires legal review. |

Keep the memo short and audit-ready: scenario, analysis (foreign national status, nature of release, carve-outs, license/exception), conclusion, AI disclosure.
