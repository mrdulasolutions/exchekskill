# Partner / Distributor Compliance: Best Practices

Reference for building a "compliance requirements for distributors/partners" pack. Covers screening expectations, re-export assurances, recordkeeping, and when to use flow-down language. Aligns with the EAR (15 C.F.R. Parts 730–774) and, where applicable, the ITAR (22 C.F.R. Parts 120–130).

---

## 1. Screening requirements (what to expect from partners)

Under the EAR, exporters and re-exporters must comply with the "steps to comply" in 15 C.F.R. § 732.4, including knowing their customer and screening parties against U.S. government lists. When you sell through distributors or partners, pushing those obligations down the chain is both a compliance and risk-management practice.

**What to require of partners:**

- **Before onboarding:** Screen the partner itself (and, if relevant, key personnel or beneficial owners) against the BIS Consolidated Screening List (CSL), OFAC SDN and other sanctions lists, and DDTC debarred parties (if ITAR items are involved). Document "no hit" or adjudicated hits before approving the relationship.
- **Per transaction:** Partners should screen each customer/end-user (and intermediate consignees, if any) before accepting orders or shipping. Screening should be run against the same lists (CSL, OFAC, DDTC as applicable). Document the screening result and retain it with the transaction.
- **Know Your Customer:** BIS "Know Your Customer" and red-flag guidance (Supplement No. 3 to Part 732) apply to re-exporters as well. Partners should be required to obtain and document end-user and end-use information and to run the red-flag checklist where appropriate. Escalation (hold, export compliance review, or legal) should be documented.

**Documentation to require:** Partners should maintain screening records (list(s) used, date, party screened, result — no hit / hit with list name and resolution). Records should be available to the supplier upon request or audit and retained per the retention period below.

**Regulatory references:** 15 C.F.R. § 732.4; BIS Consolidated Screening List; OFAC SDN and sanctions programs; 22 C.F.R. (DDTC) for ITAR; Supplement No. 3 to Part 732 (red flags).

## 2. Re-export and transfer assurances

Re-exports and in-country transfers of items subject to the EAR are subject to the EAR (15 C.F.R. § 734.2, Part 736). Partners may not re-export, transfer, or otherwise dispose of items to any country or person except as authorized by U.S. law (license, license exception, or NLR as applicable). Diversion is prohibited.

**What to require of partners:**

- **No re-export or transfer without authorization:** Partners must not re-export or transfer items (in original form or incorporated into other products) without ensuring that the re-export or transfer is permitted under the EAR (or ITAR where applicable). This includes obtaining any required re-export authorization (e.g., BIS authorization or license exception) and complying with license conditions (e.g., end-user, end-use, reporting).
- **Destination Control Statement (DCS):** For items exported under the EAR (under license or license exception), the DCS at 15 C.F.R. § 758.6 must appear on export documentation. Partners must ensure the DCS is passed along and not altered, and that they do not ship or re-export contrary to the DCS or any license condition.
- **No diversion:** Partners must not sell, transfer, or ship items to embargoed destinations, listed entities, or end-users/end-uses prohibited under the EAR or ITAR without prior U.S. government authorization. They must not facilitate diversion (e.g., via false documentation or transshipment).

**Language partners can be asked to acknowledge:** (1) They will re-export and transfer items only in compliance with U.S. export control laws and regulations. (2) They will not re-export or transfer to embargoed countries or to parties on U.S. government denial lists without required authorization. (3) They will comply with the Destination Control Statement and any license or license exception conditions. (4) They will not alter or remove export control information or the DCS.

**Regulatory references:** 15 C.F.R. §§ 734.2, 736, 758.6; 22 C.F.R. for ITAR re-exports and retransfers.

## 3. Recordkeeping

Under 15 C.F.R. Part 762, export and re-export records must be retained for at least five years from the date of export, re-export, or the last transaction under a license, whichever is later. Records must be reproducible and legible.

**What to require of partners:**

- **Records to retain:** Screening records (parties screened, list(s), date, result); export/re-export documentation (commercial invoice with export block, packing list, SLI, AES/EEI data if applicable); re-export authorizations (license exception citations or license numbers); and any correspondence or determinations related to end-user/end-use. For ITAR, 22 C.F.R. Part 122 and any agreement terms apply.
- **Retention period:** At least five years from the date of export or re-export, or from the last transaction under a license, whichever is later (15 C.F.R. § 762.6). Longer retention may be required by contract or by ITAR.
- **Format and access:** Records must be stored so they are reproducible in their original form and legible. Partners should make records available to the supplier upon reasonable request (e.g., for audit or compliance review).

**Regulatory references:** 15 C.F.R. Part 762; 22 C.F.R. Part 122 (ITAR recordkeeping).

## 4. When to use flow-down language

**Why flow down:** Contractual flow-down (1) gives the supplier a written commitment from the partner to comply with screening, re-export, and recordkeeping; (2) supports an audit trail and due diligence; (3) clarifies obligations and can support termination or remedy if the partner breaches. Many channel-heavy and OEM-focused companies require flow-down in distribution, reseller, or partner agreements.

**When to include full flow-down language:** Include a dedicated "flow-down" or "export compliance" section (or annex) with contract-ready clauses when the supplier wants to attach the same language to multiple agreements, or when legal has not yet drafted custom language. Use "sample only; recommend legal review" so that counsel can adapt to jurisdiction and contract type.

**When a summary may suffice:** If the pack is only for internal use (e.g., a one-pager for the channel team) or the supplier already has counsel-drafted flow-down, the pack can summarize requirements and reference the partner’s obligation to comply with U.S. law and the supplier’s policies, without including full contractual language. The template can offer an optional annex for flow-down language so the user can choose "include flow-down annex: Yes/No."

---

## 5. Tailoring the pack

- **Product mix:** If the supplier ships only EAR99, screening and re-export assurances still apply; recordkeeping and DCS may be simplified (e.g., NLR, no license conditions). If the supplier ships controlled ECCNs or ITAR items, the pack should explicitly reference license conditions, DCS, and ITAR retransfer/reexport rules.
- **Channel type:** For distributors (take title, resell), emphasize screening of their customers and re-export/transfer compliance. For resellers or OEMs, add end-use/end-user and integration/incorporation language as appropriate.
- **Geography:** If partners are only in low-risk countries, the pack can still require screening and recordkeeping; re-export assurances remain important for any future re-export.
